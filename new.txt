In <a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/India" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">India</a> cats were mentioned in <a href="https://www.britannica.com/art/Sanskrit-literature" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Sanskrit writings</a> around 100 <span class="text-smallcaps">bce</span>, while the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Arab" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Arabs</a> and the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Japanese</a> were not introduced to the cat until about 600 <span class="text-smallcaps">ce</span>. Since tabbylike markings appear in the drawings and <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/mummy" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">mummies</a> of ancient Egyptian cats, present-day <span id="ref41894"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/tabby" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">tabbies</a> may be <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off eb" data-term="descendants" href="https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/descendants" data-type="EB">descendants</a> of the sacred cats of Egypt. The <span id="ref41895"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/Abyssinian" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Abyssinian</a> also resembles pictures and statues of Egyptian cats. The <span id="ref41896"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/longhair" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Persian</a>, whose colouring is often the same as that of mixed breeds (although the length of hair and the body conformation are distinctive), was probably crossed at various times with other breeds. The tailless <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/Manx-cat" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Manx</a> cat, like the hairless <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/Sphynx-cat" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Sphynx cat</a> and curly-coated Devon Rex, is a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/science/mutation-genetics" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">mutation</a>. The ancestry of Persian and <span id="ref41897"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/Siamese-breed-of-cat" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Siamese cats</a> may well be distinct from that of other domestic breeds, representing a domestication of an Asian wild cat. In the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bible" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Bible</a>, cat is mentioned only in the <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off mw" data-term="apocryphal" href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/apocryphal" data-type="MW">apocryphal</a> <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Letter-of-Jeremiah" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Letter of Jeremiah</a>. The cat figured prominently in the religions of Egypt, the Norse countries, and various parts of <a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Asia</a>. The Egyptians had a cat-headed goddess named Bast (or <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bastet" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Bastet</a>). Thousands of cat <span id="ref41898"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/mummy" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">mummies</a> have been discovered in <a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Egypt" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Egypt</a>, and there were even <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/mouse-rodent" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">mouse</a> mummies, presumably to provide <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/food" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">food</a> for the cats. Often the cat has been associated with <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/sorcery" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">sorcery</a> and <span id="ref41899"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/witchcraft" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">witchcraft</a>, and the superstitions regarding cats are innumerable. Throughout the ages, cats have been more cruelly mistreated than perhaps any other <a href="https://www.britannica.com/animal/animal" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">animal</a>. Black cats in particular have long been regarded as having <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/occultism" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">occult powers</a> and as being the familiars of witches.</p><!--[P9]--><span class="marker p9"></span><!--[AM9]--><span class="marker AM9 am-inline"></span><div class="one-good-fact-module">
